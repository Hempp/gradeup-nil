# HS-NIL Brand Outreach Templates

**Purpose:** Source the 3 brand partners for the concierge MVP's first cohort (5 parents + 3 brands). You're the one sending these. Every template is written to be copy-pasted with three or four field swaps and nothing else.

**Scope:** California only. Local brands. Training facilities, tutoring companies, and local restaurants only. Deals in the $100-$500 range.

---

## Who to target

### Vertical priorities

Three verticals, in order:

1. **Training facility** — strength-and-conditioning gyms, speed-and-agility programs, sport-specific academies (pitching, shooting, swing labs). These brands already serve athletes, already understand why GPA matters to a recruiting narrative, and already pay for local-youth marketing. Highest conversion.
2. **Tutoring** — SAT/ACT prep, academic coaching, test-prep pods. The academic angle is native to their brand. A 3.8-GPA freshman is their literal target customer. Expect the shortest "it clicks" moment.
3. **Local restaurant** — family-owned spots within 5 miles of a target high school. The "scholar-athlete eating at your spot" angle is softer than the other two verticals but the story travels well on social.

**Skip for the first cohort:** fitness/supplement brands (banned-category risk, legal complexity on minors) and local retail (school-IP risk — too easy for a clothing photo to accidentally include a uniform).

### Company size

5-50 employees. Bigger means legal review will take longer than the concierge window. Smaller means no marketing budget and no one to sign the MSA.

### Geographic

Within 25 miles of your first 5 concierge parents. Pick one California metro and stay there: LA, SF, or SD. Do not split focus across metros during the concierge.

### Signal to screen for

One of these two signals must be present before you send:

- Already running Instagram ads (check their page; scroll for "Sponsored" tags they've posted, or use the Meta Ad Library).
- Already sponsoring a local youth sports team or school booster club.

If neither signal is present, deprioritize. A brand that doesn't already spend on local-youth marketing is a 3-month education sale, not a 2-week concierge close.

## Warm-intro template

When a mutual connection has offered to introduce you, send THIS to the intro-maker (not the brand):

> Hey [Name] — would love the intro to [Brand contact] at [Brand] whenever makes sense. For context you can paste: "[Founder] runs GradeUp, a platform for high-school NIL deals with 3.5+ GPA scholar-athletes. They're building a local California cohort and wanted 15 minutes to see if [Brand] might be a fit for a $100-$500 deal. Fully parent-consented and CIF-compliant." Happy to draft the forward if easier.

The two-sentence brand-facing copy inside the quotes is the forward. Your intro-maker can paste it into an email without adding a single word. That's the whole point — make it zero-effort.

## Follow-up sequence

Four touches, then stop. Silence is an answer.

- **Day 0 — initial email.** One of the three templates above. Single clear CTA: the 15-minute call.
- **Day 3 — light bump.** Reply to your own thread with one sentence: *"Hey [Name] — just making sure this didn't get buried. Worth a quick 15?"* Nothing else. No new pitch, no new info.
- **Day 7 — value-add follow-up.** Attach one thing: the FMV calculator at gradeupnil.com/solutions/brands/fmv. One sentence: *"Curious what range you'd land on here — takes 30 seconds."* Gives them something to do that isn't a meeting.
- **Day 14 — final.** One sentence: *"Last ping on this one — if NIL isn't a fit right now, no hard feelings, and I won't keep pestering you."* Sign off.

Do NOT continue past day 14. No "just checking in again in Q3" emails. If they're interested later they know where to find you. Respect "no" communicated via silence — it is a no.

## Common objections + responses

Keep responses to two sentences. If you need a third, you're losing them.

**"We don't have a NIL budget."**
> You don't need one — this comes out of your local-marketing or community-sponsorship budget, not a separate line item. A $250 athlete post is the same spend as a Yelp ad, but the story actually travels.

**"Isn't this NCAA stuff?"**
> Different league — this is high school, not college, and 41 states now permit it including California. The NCAA rules don't apply here; CIF does, and we handle that compliance for you.

**"What if the kid posts something off-brand?"**
> Every piece of creative gets pre-approved by you before it goes live — the athlete drafts, parent reviews, you sign off, then it publishes. We also run a content classifier that flags school-IP and banned-category material before it ever reaches your queue.

**"Seems too legally complex."**
> I hear you — that's exactly the thing we built the platform to take off your plate. The state rules engine, parental consent, 72-hour CIF disclosure, parent-custodial payments — that's all handled; the one-pager at gradeupnil.com/solutions/brands/compliance walks through it.

**"Can we just do this without the platform fees?"**
> Technically you could, but the parental consent, CIF disclosure, and escrow-to-parent pieces are each legal exposure if you skip them — the fee is structurally "don't get sued" insurance. Doing it off-platform is how a local brand ends up in a local paper for the wrong reason.

## What NOT to say

- **No "influencer" framing.** Ever. It's "scholar-athlete." The word influencer activates every concern the parent and the CIF compliance officer have. The word scholar-athlete activates the opposite set.
- **No specific athlete names in outreach.** Matching happens after the brand says yes. Naming athletes before consent is a privacy issue and a parent-trust issue. "We have a [sport] player at [School] near you" is as specific as you get on a cold email.
- **No promised ROI numbers.** You don't have enough deal data to back a claim like "brands see 3x engagement on scholar-athlete posts." Don't invent one. If asked, say: "We're still in the concierge phase and building that data. I can tell you anecdotally the first few have seen [X], but I'd rather underpromise here."
- **No NCAA eligibility advice.** You are not their lawyer. If they ask "will this mess up the kid's NCAA eligibility later?" the answer is: "HS NIL activity is recognized by the NCAA as long as it's compliant at the HS level, which our platform enforces — but I'd point anyone with a specific eligibility question to their school's AD or a sports lawyer."
- **No discount bargaining.** If they ask to negotiate platform fees during the concierge: no. The concierge is the validation test, not the revenue test. A brand that won't do the pilot at standard terms is not a fit for the first cohort.
