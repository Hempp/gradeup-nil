# HS-NIL Parent Intake Call Script

**Purpose:** The 15-minute Zoom call you run with every prospective concierge parent before you accept them into the 5-parent cohort. This is both an explainer and a filter. Roughly half the parents you talk to should end the call on your yellow or red list — if everyone is green, you are not asking the green-flag questions with enough bite.

**Audience:** You. Run these calls personally. Do not delegate the intake to an ops partner during the concierge — the signal you're listening for is tonal, not factual.

**Format:** 15 minutes on Zoom, video on. Phone is acceptable only if the parent is traveling. Text/email intake is not acceptable — you cannot read a parent's body language in a DM, and that's most of what you're here for.

---

## Pre-call — what to verify before accepting a call

Do not book the call until all five of these are true. Put them in a checklist next to your calendar:

1. **Parent source is known.** One of: (a) waitlist signup on the marketing page, (b) warm introduction from an AD / coach / parent in your network, (c) manual concierge-CSV import where you already know the parent personally. If the source is "I don't remember how they got in touch," don't run the call — go back and trace it first. Source matters for the referral data.
2. **Athlete graduates 2026-2029.** Freshmen through seniors. No 8th graders. No kids who already graduated. Ask this up front in the booking form; if the grad year is missing, email and ask before confirming the call.
3. **GPA ≥ 3.5 (self-reported).** This is self-reported from the waitlist signup — Tier A is fine for concierge. If the parent signed up without entering a GPA, email and ask before confirming. Parents who refuse to share the GPA before the call are a red flag and you should not book.
4. **Lives within 25 miles of your pilot-state metro.** Same metro you picked for brand sourcing (LA, SF, or SD). If the athlete is in CA but outside your metro, explain on email that the concierge is metro-local and add them to the broader waitlist.
5. **Parent has ≥300 followers on Instagram, Facebook, or LinkedIn.** The parent — not the athlete. Check before the call. If the parent has zero public social presence they cannot amplify, which means even a successful deal won't generate the loop we're testing. Drop to waitlist.

If any of the five fails, the parent goes to the post-concierge waitlist with a warm email explaining they'll hear from you again when you open the next cohort. Do not run a courtesy call on a disqualified parent — it wastes your time and teaches the wrong lesson about the bar.

## 15-min call script

Run this by the clock. If you're running long at minute 10, skip the concierge explanation deepening and jump straight to the green-flag questions — the green-flag questions are the only part of this call that actually matters.

### Minute 0-2 — Warm intro

> "Thanks for taking time, [Parent name]. I'm [Founder]. Before we go deep on anything, I want to make sure GradeUp is a fit for your family specifically — this is a short call and it's going to feel more like a conversation than a sales pitch. I'll explain what NIL is, how our concierge works, what I'd need from you, and then I've got three quick questions for you. Sound good?"

Let them say yes. Do not launch before they verbally agree to the structure. If they try to hijack with their own question, answer it briefly (30 seconds) and then return to the structure.

### Minute 2-5 — Explain NIL in 3 sentences

Do not use jargon. Do not reference college NIL stories unless they bring them up first.

> "Quick version: NIL means a student-athlete can get paid by local brands for social posts or in-person appearances. Used to be a college-only thing; 41 states now permit it at the high school level, including California. We focus specifically on scholar-athletes — kids with a 3.5 GPA or higher — because that academic piece is what makes the deals feel like a mini-scholarship instead of an influencer gig, which is the framing that makes brands and schools actually comfortable with it."

Pause. Let them ask a question. Common ones: "Is this like the college NIL thing?" or "Is this legal in California?" Answer in one sentence each and move on.

### Minute 5-8 — Explain the concierge specifically

> "Right now we're in what I call concierge mode. I'm hand-brokering every single deal myself for a small cohort — 5 families in this first group. That means there's no app for you to learn. You don't log into anything. You get a text from me when I've got a brand that looks like a fit, I send you the deal terms, you review them with [athlete name], and if you like it you sign a one-page consent and we're off. There are no platform fees during the concierge — you get 100% of the deal amount."

Key beats: hand-brokered, no app to learn, no platform fees during concierge, 100% of the deal lands with the family. Let those sink in — for many parents this is the moment the discomfort drops.

### Minute 8-10 — Explain the parent's role

> "Here's what I need from you. You sign a parental consent form — one page, takes 5 minutes. In that consent you approve the category of deals you're comfortable with — so for example, 'food and beverage up to $500, no fitness supplements, no alcohol' — and I only bring you deals inside that envelope. [Athlete name] handles the actual creative: the post, the photo, whatever the deliverable is. Payment goes directly to your bank account, not theirs. You're the custodian on the money until they're 18. That's a California requirement, and frankly it's also how you keep the IRS side of this clean."

Parent-custodial payment is the thing that surprises most parents. Pause after that sentence and let them react. If they push back on the money going to their account, that's a red flag — note it for the scoring rubric.

### Minute 10-13 — The 3 green-flag questions

This is the only part of the call that determines inclusion in the cohort. Everything before this is informational. The three questions below are non-negotiable — ask all three in this order, verbatim, and listen to how they answer, not just what they say. Tone matters more than content.

**Question 1:**

> "If [athlete name] got a $250 deal from a local gym, would you naturally tell other parents about it?"

- **Green:** "Yes, of course" — said with energy, often with a smile, sometimes with a specific person already named ("Oh, I'd tell the soccer parents in our group chat first").
- **Yellow:** "Probably, yeah" — flat tone, no specificity, agreement without engagement.
- **Red:** "I mean, maybe? Depends on who." Or worse: hesitation followed by a qualifier about "not wanting to brag."

**Question 2:**

> "Is there any chance this would feel weird for [athlete name] at school?"

- **Green:** "No, they'd be pumped. They've been talking about this for months." Confidence in the athlete's comfort.
- **Yellow:** "I'd want to ask them first." Reasonable but indicates the athlete isn't already aligned.
- **Red:** "Hmm, good question — I hadn't thought about that." That's a parent who's not actually talking to their kid about this, which means you're about to broker a deal for a kid whose buy-in is unknown.

**Question 3:**

> "Can I email you the consent doc tonight? It's one page and takes five minutes."

- **Green:** "Yes, absolutely. I'll sign it tonight." Zero friction.
- **Yellow:** "Next week is better — this week is crazy." Real reason, but real friction. Acceptable for now; the 48-hour follow-up will tell you more.
- **Red:** "Let me check with my spouse / lawyer / kid first before we sign anything." Not a fit for the concierge. A parent who needs a lawyer to review a one-page consent is a parent who's going to need a lawyer to review every deal, and the concierge cannot sustain that pace.

Do not explain the scoring rubric on the call. Record your read privately.

### Minute 13-15 — Next steps

Branch based on your read of the three green-flag questions:

**If green (3/3 or 2/3 with the consent question green):**

> "Great — I'm going to send the consent doc tonight. My aim is to have [athlete name]'s first brand introduction inside the next week. I'll text you when I find the right match; no pressure to say yes to any specific brand."

**If yellow or red:**

> "Totally understood — let me send you some info to read through on your own schedule, and we can regroup in a week or two if it still feels like a fit."

Do not reject on the call. Exit politely regardless of your read. You have the 48-hour window after the call to decide and notify via email.

## Scoring rubric

Score the three green-flag questions immediately after the call. Do it before you check email, before you take the next meeting. First-impression scoring is more accurate than reviewed scoring.

- **3/3 green → include in cohort.** Send consent doc tonight. Start brand matching within 3 days.
- **2/3 green → include but watch.** The one yellow is the risk. Send consent doc tonight. Note the risk in the ops tracker explicitly (e.g., "yellow on Q2 — athlete buy-in unconfirmed").
- **1/3 green → exclude politely.** Email within 24 hours: "We loved talking with you and we'll be in touch when we open the next cohort." Do not specify why they were excluded.
- **0/3 green → exclude immediately, no follow-up.** Polite email, no waitlist promise, and do not reopen. A 0/3 parent is not in the market for what you're building.

The rubric is deliberately strict. You are sourcing 5 parents out of 15-25 calls, not 5 out of 6. If you are accepting everyone you meet with, the rubric is not calibrated and your cohort signal will be mud.

## Red flags to exit politely

Any of these appearing on the call trumps the green-flag scoring. If you see one, the parent is a polite-decline regardless of question scores:

- **Pushing back on parental consent being required.** "Why does this need my consent — the kid is 16, they can decide." Consent is federal and state-level non-negotiable. A parent challenging it on the intake call will challenge it on every deal.
- **Asking if money can go to the athlete's account.** "Can you just pay her directly? She has her own Venmo." No, legally, and the fact that they're asking means they don't understand the structure you're building.
- **Athlete not aware of the call.** "Oh, they don't know yet — I wanted to see if this was real first." Defensible in isolation, but flag for follow-up. If after the call the parent says "I talked to them and they're not into it," that's normal teenage ambivalence, not a deal-breaker — re-score question 2 with the new data.
- **Asking about tax implications before asking about the deal itself.** Not red by itself — plenty of thoughtful parents ask about taxes. It's red when it's the first or second question and there's no follow-up curiosity about the brand, the athlete, or the story. That's a parent treating this as a side-hustle spreadsheet, not a family moment.
- **Any hint of living through the athlete's success.** "They NEED this — this could be their scholarship." "We've been waiting for something like this for their future." The wedge is parents who are proud of their kid, not parents whose identity depends on the kid's monetization. This is the single hardest signal to read and the single most important one to listen for. If you feel it, trust it.

## Post-call checklist

Run this inside 60 minutes of the call ending. Do not batch it to end-of-day — you will forget details.

1. **Send the consent doc** (if green/yellow). Phase 1 flow lives at `/hs/consent/request` once the feature flag is on for the parent's account. During the concierge it is acceptable to send a manually reviewed PDF instead — faster and gives you more control over edge-case phrasing.
2. **Add the parent to the ops tracker** (the Google Sheet from the 5-Parent Playbook). Columns to fill today: source, call date, green-flag count, decision (include / watch / exclude), notes. Don't wait on the missing fields; fill what you have.
3. **Schedule a 48-hour follow-up** if the consent doc has not been signed by then. One email, one sentence: "Hey [Name] — just flagging that the consent is still open; let me know if you want me to walk through any of it." Nothing more.
4. **Kick off brand matching** within 3 days of consent signing. Open the brand board, find 2-3 brands whose target profile matches [athlete], and reach out using the outreach template doc.

## What I WON'T promise on this call

Memorize this list. It is the list of things parents will try to get you to commit to, and committing is how the concierge ends badly.

- **Guaranteed deals.** You cannot guarantee a deal. What you can commit to is that you will try — "I'm aiming to have a brand introduction inside a week, and if I can't find a fit in two weeks I'll tell you honestly" is the strongest commitment you should make.
- **Minimum dollar amounts.** Do not say "at least $250." The deal range depends on the brand, the athlete's following, and the deliverable. Give the $100-$500 range as informational, not as a floor.
- **Timeline for first deal beyond "within two weeks."** If pressed for specifics: "within two weeks — if I can't hit that I'll tell you and we'll decide whether to keep going or pause."
- **NCAA eligibility opinions.** If a parent asks "will this hurt their chances at a D1 scholarship?", the answer is: "HS NIL activity is recognized by the NCAA as long as it's compliant at the HS level, which is exactly what the platform enforces. I'd point anyone with a specific recruiting question to a sports lawyer or their school's athletic director." Do not extrapolate.
- **Tax advice.** "The parent-custodial structure keeps things clean on the IRS side, but I can't give tax advice — your accountant can walk through the 1099 when the deal closes." That's the whole answer. Never more.
